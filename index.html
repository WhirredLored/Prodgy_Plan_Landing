The problem

The current system is failing three people at once.

When a pet gets sick and a family can't afford care, there's a story most of the industry wants to tell about personal responsibility and planning ahead. That story is incomplete. The truth is that the financing infrastructure around veterinary care is broken, and it's breaking down in three different places at the same time.

1

The pet parent gets a bill they can't plan for and options designed against them.

Pet insurance works on the same math as any insurance product: most of the time, most customers pay more in premiums than they ever receive in claims. When claims do happen, exclusions and reimbursement structures often leave families paying thousands out of pocket anyway. Medical credit cards — the default option when insurance isn't in place — are deferred-interest products that can charge six months of retroactive interest at rates approaching 27% if the balance isn't paid in full within a promotional window most people don't understand at signing. Neither product is built for the pet parent. Both are built around the pet parent.

2

The independent clinic absorbs risk it wasn't built to carry.

Small veterinary practices operate on thin cash reserves and unpredictable case mix. When a family can't pay, the clinic's only real options are to turn the animal away, extend informal credit that often doesn't get repaid, or push clients toward third-party financing products the clinic quietly resents. Emergency care in particular is hard to justify staying open for when the math stops working — and the math stops working a lot more often than most people outside the industry realize.

3

The animal gets caught in the middle.

When financing fails, care doesn't get delayed; it gets declined. A pet that would have received treatment under almost any other circumstance goes home without it — or doesn't go home at all. That outcome isn't the fault of the family that loves the animal, and it isn't the fault of the vet who wants to help. It's the fault of infrastructure that was never designed for the situation it finds itself handling.

The alternative

Your vet is the point. We're the infrastructure underneath.

Every pet insurance company wants to sit between you and your veterinarian. Every pet-tech app wants to pull your attention into their portal, their brand, their relationship. We don't. Your clinic keeps its name, its brand, and its relationship with you.

What Prodgy Pet Plans provides is the financial and operational infrastructure that lets your vet offer something better than a credit card application in a crisis — an affordable, predictable care plan with financing that's extended based on how you show up at the clinic, not what a credit bureau has on file.

Missed a credit card payment three years ago? Doesn't matter here. Paid your last six visits on time and brought your dog in for her wellness checks? That's what we care about. That's what we use.

No insurance premiums. No credit checks. No third-party lender getting in the way of the relationship between you and the people who take care of your animal.

About the founder

Why I'm building this.

A few years ago, my dog Loonuhh got very sick, very fast. The emergency hospital I drove her to turned us away because I couldn't pay the deposit up front. A second clinic I found that afternoon didn't. Her surgery happened that night. She came home the next morning.

That experience could have been the end of the story — one family, one dog, one close call. What made it the beginning of a company instead was learning that the pattern I'd just lived through wasn't unusual. It's what happens, quietly, to families and clinics across the country every week.

I'm not a veterinarian. I'm not a financial services executive. I'm a solo founder with a background in construction, a pretty good understanding of systems, and the conviction that the financial infrastructure around veterinary care has needed serious work for a long time. Prodgy Pet Plans is what I've been building about it.

— Jared
